The Mission

Fix the Measurement Problem.
Not the Industry.

The global call for change says business events are incredibly damaging to the environment. And when every flight, every product, every supply chain impact is tallied and attributed to the event organizer — of course it looks that way. The current model holds planners responsible for emissions they don’t own, asks them to reduce attendance travel that isn’t theirs to control, and then calls it a sustainability program.

Events don’t need to change. The reporting model does. It hasn’t been meaningfully updated since the first sustainability guidance was introduced in the early 2000s. The frameworks are real — ISO 20121, EIC, NZCE — and the industry leaders who built them did extraordinary work. What was always missing was the connective reporting infrastructure to bring it all together and make it usable at scale.

That is what Green Analytics provides. Not another consultant. Not another guide. Just better data, faster — the updated measurement model that finally makes all the other pieces line up.

The Foundation

Three Standards.
One System.

Every claim in the GA methodology traces to one of three governing frameworks. No proprietary science — just the industry's own standards, applied consistently.

Events Industry Council (EIC)

The EIC defines what constitutes a business event and establishes the economic scope of each contracted space — who participates, what is contracted, and what falls within the official event boundary. It is the industry’s authoritative standard for scoping professional events.

Net Zero Carbon Events (NZCE)

The global industry roadmap for emissions reduction, developed by event industry leaders worldwide. NZCE identifies five priority action areas and establishes the directional standard: reduce emissions at the source first, use offsets only for the unavoidable residual.

LEED — US Green Building Council

The world’s most widely used green building rating system. LEED has established decades of precedent for how to document and verify built environment performance — energy consumption, materials tracking, supply chain reporting, and ASHRAE-level mechanical system detail.

GA reports are also structured for compatibility with GHG Protocol (Scope 3.6 Business Travel attribution), CDP, GRI, and CSRD corporate disclosure requirements — so attendee emissions are correctly attributed to the employer, and corporate sustainability teams can use all outputs directly without rework.

Ten Years in the Making

The Question That
Started Everything

“In 2012, ISO 20121, APEX/ASTM, and USGBC guidance all landed at once. The frameworks were real — and we implemented them as well as anyone could. But I would look out across that venue calendar and watch dozens of events cycle through the same space every month — and wonder why only a handful were even trying. Not because the guidance was wrong. Because something structural wasn’t working.”

Ryan Green, Founder — Green Analytics

That question became a decade-long research mission. Not to build another program — but to understand why engagement was stuck at 3% despite an abundance of guidance, and why implementation was so complex that even the most committed planners struggled to sustain it year over year. Moving through every seat at the industry table — venue, non-profit planning team, exhibitor, contractor, national operations — the same three roadblocks appeared everywhere: cost, complexity, and a reporting model that hadn’t been meaningfully updated since sustainability guidance was first introduced in the early 2000s.

When the NZCE launched its global call for systemic change, the instinct of the 97% was understandable. The framing implied that events themselves were the problem — that flights needed to be reduced, that supply chains needed to be rebuilt, that planners needed to own and reduce every emission source in the full event footprint. For a profession already operating at capacity, that message landed as more burden, not more clarity.

But the diagnosis was wrong. The industry wasn’t broken. The measurement model was. Twenty years of guidance, from some of the most knowledgeable people in the business, had produced frameworks that were technically sound and practically unusable — because the connective reporting infrastructure that would make them work together had never been built. That is what Green Analytics is. Not a new consultant. Not a new guide. The reporting layer that finally makes all the existing pieces make sense.
