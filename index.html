Why 3% Is Not a Surprise

The Industry Ignored
Green Guidance
For Good Reason.

Decades of sustainability frameworks asked planners to set their own scope, implement their own programs, and self-certify their own results — with no measurement standard, no funding model, and no compensation for the work. Burnout was inevitable. When engagement flatlined, the NZCE launched a global push to re-engage the industry. But planners looked at it and saw more of the same: heavy, complex, planner-owned guidance that held organizers responsible for emissions they don’t control. So 97% walked away. Green Analytics is what changes that calculus.

1

Self-implementation burned planners out

ISO 20121 and earlier frameworks let planners set their own scope — which sounds flexible but means no standard, no comparability, and no way to prove anything. Planners did the work and got nothing usable at the end.

2

Reporting became theater, not measurement

Waste diversion headlines, water usage graphs, one-off vendor pledges. Easy to photograph, impossible to compare, useless to corporate sustainability teams trying to meet real disclosure requirements.

3

The industry’s real strengths stayed invisible

Permanent venues, rental ecosystems, reuse models, consolidated logistics — the structural efficiencies that already make business events low-impact were never measured. Planners got no credit for any of it.

85%

of an event’s total footprint comes from attendee travel and exhibitor operations — neither of which the organizer controls. Previous frameworks assigned all of it to the planner anyway.
